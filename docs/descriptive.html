Descriptive statistics for the anti-criminalization activity index (crim_activity_index) across FY2022, FY2023, and FY2024 — with an explicit view into what shifted after the June 2024 Supreme Court ruling in City of Grants Pass v. Johnson.

Grants Pass as the policy shock

City of Grants Pass v. Johnson (603 U.S. ___, decided June 28, 2024) held 6–3 that enforcing public-camping ordinances against people experiencing homelessness does not constitute cruel and unusual punishment under the Eighth Amendment. The decision overruled the Ninth Circuit's Martin v. Boise (2019) framework that had effectively blocked anti-camping enforcement in nine western states when shelter space was unavailable.

The FY2024 HUD CoC Application deadline was October 30, 2024 — four months after the ruling. FY2024 reports therefore capture CoCs' first documented anti-criminalization posture after the legal landscape shifted. The design exploits this timing as a quasi-exogenous shock.

Distribution shift

Each bar shows the share of CoCs landing in each discrete value of the activity index within a given year. Two things are visible:

  • The 0.50 bucket shrinks in FY2024 (FY22 38% → FY23 39% → FY24 15%): fewer CoCs end up at the "half-yes" level.
  • The 1.00 (all-Yes) bucket balloons in FY2024 (FY22 39% → FY23 43% → FY24 58%): more CoCs report maximal anti-criminalization activity.

This is a bimodal shift — CoCs aren't incrementally adding one more Yes; they're jumping from "half" to "all". Consistent with an instrument-change story (new FY24 form items are easier to check "Yes" to) layered on top of whatever substantive post-Grants Pass response exists.

Why this matters for interpretation: the "Implementation" column was labeled differently in each form:

  • FY2022/23: "Reverse existing criminalization policies" (what you must have undone)
  • FY2024: "Implemented Laws/Policies/Practices that Prevent Criminalization" (what you have implemented)

FY2024's wording is substantially easier to check "Yes" to — any adopted policy, even one existing for years, qualifies. FY2022/23's wording required evidence that an existing criminalization policy was reversed, which is much more stringent. What we're actually observing

The +0.083 FY23→FY24 jump in the overall activity index is partly an instrument artifact, partly a real response to Grants Pass, and in any case affects nonprofit-led and government-led CoCs equivalently. The "Policymaker engagement" column — which asked essentially the same question across all three years — actually declined slightly in FY2024 (~3-point drop). The increase is concentrated in the "Implementation" column, whose wording changed in ways that mechanically make "Yes" easier to report.

This is why the paper should:

  1. Report cell-level descriptives to make the instrument effect transparent;
  2. Rely on DiD (which differences out common shifts) rather than raw year-on-year comparisons;
  3. Treat the DiD null as the primary finding — both groups moved identically, so even after the instrument change, leadership type does not predict differential response.

Only 38% of CoCs actually raised their score FY23→FY24. The +0.095 mean increase is driven by a subset making large jumps while 41% stayed exactly the same — a signature consistent with the HUD 1D-4 form redesign producing a step-function shift for CoCs that updated their reporting practices, rather than a gradual substantive behavior change.

Bottom line

  • Pre-period (FY22 → FY23): roughly stable activity index, with nearly parallel trajectories for nonprofit and government CoCs.
  • Post-Grants Pass (FY23 → FY24): both groups jump by ~0.08 points. The jump is concentrated in the "Implementation" cells whose wording changed to be easier to check Yes.
  • No differential response by lead-agency type (DiD ≈ 0, pboot = 0.819). The theoretical prediction that nonprofit-led CoCs should more aggressively reject criminalization is not supported.
  • The descriptive story is consistent with — but does not prove — an instrument-change interpretation. External behavioral data (NLHR ordinance counts, FBI UCR vagrancy arrests) are the next step to validate whether the FY2024 jump reflects real behavior change.
