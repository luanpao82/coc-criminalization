**From Inclusion to Influence: How CoC Governance Shapes Engagement with
People with Lived Experience and Local Responses to Homelessness
Criminalization**

**Research Question and Motivation**\
This study examines how Continuums of Care (CoCs) engage people with
lived experience (PLE) of homelessness and whether such engagement
shapes local policy and enforcement responses to homelessness. CoCs are
central governance structures in local homelessness systems,
coordinating service providers, setting priorities, and influencing how
homelessness is addressed in practice. Although federal guidance
increasingly emphasizes the importance of including PLE in planning and
decision-making, the degree to which CoCs institutionalize such
engagement likely varies substantially across communities. This
variation may matter not only for representation, but also for
substantive outcomes---particularly whether communities adopt punitive
responses to homelessness, such as anti-camping ordinances, fines, and
other forms of criminalization.

This study asks three questions:

(1) Does the leadership structure of a CoC---particularly whether it is
    led primarily by a nonprofit organization or a governmental
    entity---shape the extent to which PLE are included in governance
    and decision-making?;

(2) Are higher levels of PLE engagement associated with less punitive
    local homelessness policies?; and

(3) Does stronger PLE engagement correspond to lower levels of
    homelessness-related law enforcement?

**Theoretical Framework**\
The project brings together scholarship on collaborative governance,
participatory inclusion, and the criminalization of homelessness. This
study argues that CoC leadership structure shapes the architecture of
participation, including whether PLE are included on governing boards,
advisory bodies, or routine consultation processes. In turn, more
meaningful and institutionalized PLE engagement may shift local
responses away from punishment and exclusion and toward service-oriented
or rights-based approaches. In this framework, PLE engagement functions
as both a democratic good and a mechanism that may influence policy
outputs and enforcement practices.

**Data and Measures**\
The project draws on **annual HUD CoC application materials** to build
original measures of governance structure and PLE engagement (see
below). These documents provide information on lead agency type, board
composition, decision-making arrangements, and whether PLE are
represented in formal governance processes. From these materials, we
will create a PLE engagement index capturing the presence, frequency,
and institutional depth of participation.

We will link these data to **local ordinances** related to the
criminalization of homelessness, including anti-camping bans,
restrictions on public sleeping, panhandling regulations, and associated
penalties such as fines. Where feasible, we will also collect **local
law-enforcement data**, including citations or arrests associated with
homelessness-related offenses and records identifying transient or
unhoused status.

**Research Design**\
Using a comparative dataset across CoCs, I will test whether
nonprofit-led and government-led CoCs differ in the extent of PLE
engagement and whether higher engagement is associated with less
punitive policy and enforcement outcomes. Depending on data
availability, the study will use cross-sectional or panel models and
control for factors such as CoC size, local homelessness rates,
urbanicity, housing market pressure, and political context.

**Contribution**\
This project contributes to public administration and homelessness
governance research in three ways. First, it conceptualizes CoCs as
institutions of participatory governance rather than merely service
coordination bodies. Second, it develops an original and measurable
framework for assessing the institutionalization of lived-experience
engagement. Third, it connects PLE inclusion to concrete policy and
enforcement outcomes, showing that participation may shape not only who
is heard, but also how homelessness is governed.

  1\. The CoC gathers feedback from persons with lived experience by
  encouraging participation in CoC meetings and working groups and
  through feedback forms provided to both current and recent clients. 2.
  The CoC routinely gathers feedback from current and recent clients
  through a feedback form created by the lead agency. This form gives
  clients an opportunity to anonymously provide input on our current
  services as well as suggestions for additional services that would
  better meet the needs of clients. This form is provided during
  outreach projects and community resource fairs. Client feedback allows
  for immediate needs to be addressed and also provides a wider set of
  data to be used for strategic planning. 3. The Rapid Rehousing
  Feedback Form requests suggestions for improvements and additional
  services that may allow us to better serve the needs of future clients
  in CoC and ESG programs. 4. This form is provided to clients 6 months
  after receiving assistance through our ESG RRH program or when a
  client exits the program. This form allows our CoC to gather feedback
  on what services did and did not help them achieve their goals of
  stable permanent housing. 5. The CoC has implemented changes to our
  intake process, street outreach, Community Resource Centers/CE Access
  Points, and many other programs to address challenges raised by people
  with lived experience. The CoC has created access points at various
  locations throughout our catchment area as well as allowing our case
  managers to travel to clients without transportation to an access
  point. We have updated our intake form to remove invasive/unnecessary
  questions. Our CoC has also invited guest providers to the Community
  Resource Centers/CE Access Points to provide services frequently
  requested by our clients on site for a one-stop-shop approach. The CoC
  has future plans for developing and distributing customer satisfaction
  surveys that will be made available to all CoC-program participants
  and not just recent clients. These surveys will be available at
  service agencies and online allowing for anonymous submissions. The
  goal is to utilize candid feedback to assess the daily experiences of
  clients participating in CoC-funded programs. This data will be used
  to measure successes and identify areas for further improvement,
  trauma-informed training, better data collection, or areas for
  increased collaboration.
